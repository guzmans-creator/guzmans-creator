Sketch Design

Started by selecting two public STL models, I selected Low-Poly Plastic Cup Free 3D Model - .x3d .x .wrl .unity .tbscene .stl .raw .ply .obj .gltf .fbx .dxf .dae .abc .3ds .blend - Free3D, and Desktop Cable Holder - Adhesive or Screw Mount - Free 3D Print Model - MakerWorld in order to create a desk organizer that tripled as a writing utensil holder, cable holder and phone stand. See Figure Sketch.

TinkerCAD Settings

Next, open Tinkercad and create a new design. Click import at the top right, select the STL file from your computer (or drag and drop it), choose the scale if prompted but it is usually kept on default, and click the import to add the STL to the workspace. Repeat for any additional STL files. See Figure 1. In Tinkercad, I edited the models by adding two squares that would act as the phone stand then grouped them together(located at the top right corner when everything you want to group together is selected). After finalizing the design, I exported it as an STL file from Tinkercad.

Prusa Slicer Settings

Next, the STL file was opened in PrusaSlicer, where all print settings were configured and documented. See Figure 1.2. This included verifying the printer profile for the Prusa MINI+, selecting PLA as the filament material, and adjusting key print parameters such as layer height, infill density, print speed, and support settings(in my case the settings were pre-set so no manual adjustment was needed on my end).

Prusa Slicer Settings

Once these settings were confirmed, the model was sliced to generate the toolpath. The object was then printed on a Prusa MINI+ using PLA filament. See Figure 1.3.

TinkerCAD Remix FInal Product

The 3D desk organizer design itself was decent, but it had four major flaws. First, the overall size of the desk organizer was too small, which meant it could not realistically hold as many writing utensils as intended. Second, the phone stand was too thin and narrow, making it weak and unstable to properly support the weight of a phone. Third, the cable holder was positioned too far inside the cup and was too narrow, preventing cables from fitting correctly. Additionally, the strip of the cable holder protruded into the inside of the cup, reducing usable space. Overall, many adjustments will be made in Onshape, and some features may be removed to improve functionality and printability.See Figure 1.4 and Figure 1.4b.
