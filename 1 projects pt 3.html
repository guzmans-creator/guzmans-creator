Calibration “Torture Test”

Firstly, download an STL file that would push the limits of the machine; in my case, I chose the 3D helix. Unlike a 3MF file, which can store multiple objects, colors, and printer-specific settings, STL files only contain the raw 3D geometry of a model, making them more universal but less flexible for customization in the slicer. Next, I sliced and printed my test print, following the procedure described in Project 1: Part 1 - Prusa Slicer Software and Prusa MINI+ Guide - How To 3D Print A Name Tag. Upon completion and examination, I found that the foundation of my helix had gaps, which is an indication of under-extrusion—when the printer does not extrude enough plastic, failing to fill in the gaps between perimeters and infill. I also observed stringing or oozing, which are small strands of leftover filament, most likely caused by the nozzle moving between different sections of the print. See figure 4.

This experiment demonstrates that 3D printers are in fact capable of producing complex geometries. There are, however, practical limitations in terms of extrusion accuracy, fine detail, and material flow. Even if a 3D model can be designed digitally without restriction, the physical printer may not be able to reproduce every aspect perfectly. See Figure 4.2.
